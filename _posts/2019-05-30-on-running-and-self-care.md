title: On running and self-care
---

I like to run a lot, and the short answer I’ve always had for why is that it’s fun and it’s sort of an excuse to go outside and explore.

But that’s not quite the full answer, and the full answer is something I’m still learning to be more open about with both myself and with others. A more complete answer is that I’ve largely also been using running as a tool to help me cope with my mental health challenges. Over the past few years, coinciding with struggling through some personal anxiety and depression, trauma, and stress, I started gradually experimenting with running longer distances. Running longer and longer distances, and setting aside specific time in my busy schedule for runs has been crucial in helping me take care of myself.

At the most challenging times for my mental health, it can become very difficult for me to make decisions, see my way out of tough feelings, and do the everyday work required to keep moving on. But running helps me a lot. Sometimes, it takes all the effort in the world to get over my stress, anxiety, feels, to step out of bed, to put on my shoes, to go outside in the cold. But running helps. Because all I have to do is put one foot in front of the other, and not worry about being fast or being strong. Just gotta put one foot in front of the other, and keep on going.

And so I recently ran a marathon. This had been a long-term goal for me ever since I had started first getting into running. Always distant, always something I intended to do in the future and never quite an immediate reality. But last summer, as I started evaluating my progress on running and scheduling the required preparation work with my grad schedule, things started to line up and I decided to make the plunge.

And it was hard and tough. Not just the actual day, but all the weeks leading up to it. Finding the motivation to stick with my plan, push myself harder to increase my distance, going just a bit further than what I thought I could physically or mentally accomplish at the end of every week.

And the actual race day felt absolutely *empowering* in several ways. Empowering in getting to see my city from a whole new perspective, and empowering in all the strangers and friends cheering me on with encouraging or silly signs. Empowering in the feeling that so much of the city came out to celebrate with me and cheer a whole bunch of strangers taking part in a difficult run. LA is a city with several aspects that still make me feel uncomfortable after all these years of living here, but in this moment it all felt like home. And finally, empowering in being able to make this run happen despite my personal challenges.

The entire marathon experience felt like making the plunge to take charge of my own mental wellbeing, my self-care. Pushing until the end by putting one foot in front of the other. And just being able to get going every day of training by putting on my running shoes, tying up my laces, and starting to run outside. I’m very proud of myself for achieving this big challenge from something that I’ve largely developed as a tool for self-care and to take better control of my own mental heath.
